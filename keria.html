Keria

From AgentTV, the serious source for esports. Last updated May 7, 2026 by AGENT staff.

Ryu Min-seok (Korean: 류민석; born February 9, 2003), better known as Keria, is a South Korean professional League of Legends player who plays support for T1 in the LCK. He is a three-time League of Legends World Champion (2023, 2024, 2025) and is widely considered the best support player of his generation.[1][2]

Keria debuted with DRX in 2020 at the age of 17 and joined T1 ahead of the 2021 season. Alongside teammates Faker, Zeus, Oner and Gumayusi, he formed the core of the T1 roster that won three consecutive World Championships from 2023 to 2025.[2]

Early life and gaming background

Ryu Min-seok was born on February 9, 2003, in South Korea. He grew up inside the Korean League of Legends amateur scene of the late 2010s, a hyper-competitive solo-queue and academy ecosystem that funnelled the country's most-promising young players into the LCK pipeline before they had finished secondary school. He was identified as a top support prospect by the time he was sixteen and entered formal scouting conversations with multiple LCK organisations.[1]

His amateur reputation was built around an unusually wide champion pool for a young support, including comfort on engage tanks, enchanters and roaming mages. The combination convinced DragonX to give him a starting spot ahead of the 2020 Spring split as one of the youngest players ever to start in the LCK, a decision that paid off almost immediately.

Competitive career

DRX debut (2020)

Keria debuted on DRX as a seventeen-year-old support and was named to the All-Pro team in his rookie split. The roster qualified for the 2020 World Championship, where they were eliminated in the quarterfinals by Damwon Gaming. The brief but high-impact rookie season was enough to make him the most coveted free agent at the support position heading into the offseason, and T1 secured him on a long-term contract before the 2021 calendar opened.[2]

T1 early years (2021 to 2022)

Keria joined T1 alongside Faker for the 2021 season as the cornerstone of a generational youth project that the organisation had been preparing for several years. The lineup struggled with consistency early but built toward a historic 2022. T1 went 18-0 in the regular season of LCK Spring 2022, the first undefeated split in league history, and the team's bottom-lane synergy became the most-discussed structural piece of the roster.[2]

T1 reached the Worlds 2022 final in San Francisco, where they lost 2-3 to DRX in a five-game series that is widely cited as one of the greatest finals in League of Legends history. The defeat hardened the roster around a long-term project: rather than rebuild, T1 chose to keep the entire starting five together, betting that the youth core would mature into a multi-Worlds-winning unit.

2023 to 2025 three-peat

Keria was a foundational piece of T1's three consecutive World Championship victories. In 2023, on home soil at the Gocheok Sky Dome in Seoul, T1 defeated Weibo Gaming 3-0 in the final, ending the organisation's seven-year wait for a fifth title. The 2024 final in London saw T1 defeat Bilibili Gaming 3-2 at The O2 Arena in a closer rematch with a Chinese roster, and in 2025 T1 defeated KT Rolster 3-1 to complete the three-peat, the first such run since SKT's 2015 and 2016 back-to-back titles.[2][3]

Across the three-peat, Keria was named to multiple LCK All-Pro teams and was regularly cited by analysts as the highest-skilled engage support of his era. His champion pool through the run grew to include comfort picks ranging from Rakan and Nautilus on the engage axis to enchanters like Renata Glasc and aggressive mage supports such as Senna and Brand, and the team's draft flexibility around him became one of T1's most important structural advantages.

Recent form (2026)

The opening months of 2026 saw T1 enter the post-three-peat era as the consensus benchmark for international play. Keria has remained the undisputed central support of the LCK, and his individual numbers in MSI 2026 preparation events have continued to set the comparison line for elite support play across all four major regions.

Playstyle and analysis

Keria is best understood as a roaming, vision-first engage support whose impact is measured outside the bot lane more than inside it. His pathing in the early game tends to look unconventional on the live broadcast, with frequent rotations into the river and tri-bush before two-item powerspikes. The trade-off is a slightly weaker lane-phase economy than peers, which T1's drafts compensate for by pairing him with marksmen comfortable in the safe-lane scaling pattern. His shotcalling presence on T1 is generally framed as a quiet authority: he and Faker control the team's macro tempo together, with Faker setting the mid-game timing and Keria controlling the vision posture that lets the timing land. Coaches who have studied his demos point to his ward-trade efficiency in the 8-to-14 minute window as the single most repeatable aspect of his game, and his willingness to flash for kill-pressure in T1's protect-the-carry compositions has been the difference in several of the team's biggest series.

Notable moments

  • Named All-Pro at the support position as a seventeen-year-old in his rookie split with DRX.[2]
  • Helped T1 to an 18-0 regular season in LCK Spring 2022, the first undefeated split in league history.[2]
  • Reached the Worlds 2022 final in San Francisco, falling 2-3 to DRX in one of the most-watched finals in the game's history.[2]
  • Won the 2023 World Championship at the Gocheok Sky Dome in Seoul, T1's fifth Worlds title.[3]
  • Won back-to-back World Championships in 2024 (London) and 2025 to complete the first three-peat in League of Legends since SKT's 2015 and 2016 run.[3]
  • Holds multiple LCK titles in addition to his three Worlds rings.[2]

Legacy and impact

Keria is widely regarded as the best support player in the world and one of the greatest supports in League of Legends history. As of May 2026 he holds three Worlds titles, multiple LCK titles and is among the youngest players ever to claim three Worlds rings. His three-peat with T1 has reframed how analysts evaluate role-by-role longevity in modern League of Legends, and his impact on the support position has been compared with the influence Mata held over the role during the early-to-mid 2010s.[2]

Within T1 he occupies an unusual place in the organisation's identity. He is the structural bridge between the Faker-anchored core of the late 2010s and the youth-driven roster that won the three-peat, and his role as a quiet co-shotcaller alongside Faker has become a familiar talking point in coaching content across the LCK and other regions.

Personal life

Keria is based in Seoul and lives at the T1 player house. He is publicly close with Faker and frequently appears in T1 organisational content, including studio breakdowns and bootcamp documentary segments. He maintains an active presence on Instagram and X, where he posts predominantly in Korean.[1]
