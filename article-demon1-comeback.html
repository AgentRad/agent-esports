Demon1's Quiet Comeback After the EG Breakup

Max Mazanov was 21 when his Champions-winning roster came apart. He has spent the last two years putting the career back together piece by piece, on a quieter team, in a quieter way, and the play is starting to look like the play that won a world championship.
By Jordan Romero · Senior Valorant Reporter · 9 min read · Published April 4, 2026

The hardest months of Demon1's career, by his own account, were not the months immediately following the Champions win. They were the months a year later, when the roster that had carried him to the trophy was no longer the roster, and his name kept appearing in tier list YouTube videos in a tier most fans had not put him in twelve months earlier. The decline narrative, as he watched it form in real time, did not match what he was working on in scrim. The narrative just moved faster than the work.

Two years on, the comeback that did not have a press release attached to it is finally legible. Demon1 is, by the basic measures of duelist play, back. Not all the way back to Champions form. That version of the player was, by his own admission, a confluence of role, team structure, and a developmental moment that does not repeat. But the version that exists in 2026 is good enough to win in the modern VCT, and it is the version of the player who has done the longest unglamorous work in his career to get here.

What the EG run actually was

The 2023 Evil Geniuses Champions run was, in the moment, almost universally read as a Cinderella story. A roster nobody had pegged as a contender, no veteran star outside of jawgemo, a 21-year-old duelist whose big-stage experience was measurably thin, ran the bracket and beat Paper Rex in the final. The win was, in a real sense, structural. EG's coaching staff had identified a duelist-flexible reading of the post-Episode 7 meta and built every map veto and round structure around the version of Demon1 that thrived inside it. The combination produced a tournament MVP run.

"The thing nobody got was that the roster was the meta. We weren't surviving the patch. We had set up exactly for the patch." · Demon1, on the AgentTV Valorant podcast, December 2024

That structure broke when the meta moved. The first half of 2024 was a different patch cycle. The duelist-pressure offense EG had built was no longer the optimal version of the game. The roster was slow to adjust. By the time the off-season arrived, several core members had already begun discussions with other orgs. By spring, the roster was effectively over.

The two years that followed

What happened to Demon1 in the immediate post-breakup is the part of the story most fans tracked. The early signing rumors. The brief stint at a roster that did not work out. A return to streaming. A series of public statements, some more measured than others. A dip in the team's collective rating, then in the player's individual rating. A handful of online tournaments where the play looked, by his standards, off.

22
Months between Demon1's Champions MVP run and his next deep playoff finish at a Tier 1 international event · the longest gap of any Champions MVP in VCT history

What did not get covered, because it does not produce headlines, is the developmental work. Demon1 has been, throughout the 22 months since the breakup, doing the kind of work that does not generate clip content. He restructured his aim training routine. He moved off the duelist role for a stretch and back onto it. He took notes from coaches at three different rosters before settling on the current one. The work shows in the game now in a way that does not call attention to itself. He is reading rounds more like a four-year veteran. He is taking fewer hero duels and winning more support duels. The HLTV-equivalent rating numbers have, this season, climbed back into the top 30 across all VCT regions.

The version of the player that exists now

The clearest difference between 2023 Demon1 and 2026 Demon1 is in the round-shaping. The 2023 version played the duelist slot as a leverage role. The team gave him space and he generated mid-round momentum by winning the first contact. The 2026 version is more conservative on entries and more accurate on retakes. He is, by the eye-test, a better post-plant duelist than he was at his peak. The first-blood numbers are not what they were, but the round-win contribution numbers, by various advanced metrics, have improved.

The other change is psychological, and it shows up in interviews more than in tape. Demon1 in 2023 talked like a player who knew he was good and was waiting for the result that would prove it. Demon1 in 2026 talks like a player who has lost the result, found out he was still good, and stopped needing the result to prove it. That is a version of the player that, in theory, can keep playing at this level for longer. Burnout in duelists is largely a function of self-pressure. He is, by his own description, applying less of it.

What the comeback means

The cleanest way to read Demon1's 2026 is as a refutation of the way fans tend to write off post-peak players. The decline narrative around the Champions roster was built on the assumption that the structural advantages that had produced the title were inseparable from the player. That assumption was, in part, correct. The player is not going to win a Champions MVP in the same form. It was also, in part, incorrect. The player is good enough, in a different form, to be a Tier 1 contributor for several more seasons.

"I'm not chasing the version of me from Champions. That version doesn't exist anymore. The current version is fine. It might be enough. I think it is." · Demon1, AgentTV interview, March 2026

For the rest of the league, what Demon1's last two years should adjust is the speed at which star players are written off after a roster collapse. The recovery curve, in this case, was 22 months. It was visible only to the player himself for most of them. The output is now legible again. The career is not over. It just took a while.
