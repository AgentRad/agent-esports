Paper Rex's Pacific Breakthrough at Masters Toronto

For four years, the Pacific region was the test case for whether structural difference could survive contact with EMEA tape work. In Toronto, Paper Rex stopped being a test case and became the answer.
By Jordan Romero · Senior Valorant Reporter · 13 min read · Published April 12, 2026

The Toronto crowd had not, by the third map of the grand final, decided whose team this was. Half the lower bowl was wearing pink. The other half was wearing the dark green of an EMEA powerhouse that had spent the last two days losing rounds in the way that you lose rounds when nothing you have prepared still applies. Every time Paper Rex ran a default that did not look like a default, the camera caught a member of the EMEA bench writing something down. Most of them stopped writing in the second half. There was nothing left to copy.

What Paper Rex did at Masters Toronto is the kind of thing the Pacific scene had been promising since Riot built the franchised structure around it. The team did not just win. It won by playing a version of the game the rest of the world had spent four years deciding was not optimal. The proof finally landed. The pink wave is now, on the global stage, undeniable.

The Pacific question

Since the inaugural VCT Pacific season, the regional question has been the same. The play style produced by APAC scrim culture · faster, looser, more reliant on duelist-side aggression and individual reads · was, by EMEA and Americas standards, structurally undisciplined. EMEA teams playing Pacific opposition tended to enter international events as favorites. Pacific teams responded, sometimes, with deep runs at single events, but the consensus held. The region produced individuals. It did not, the argument went, produce champions.

"We never wanted to play their game. We wanted to make them play ours. That was the whole plan. We just hadn't won the final yet." · Alecks, Paper Rex head coach, post-match press conference, Toronto

Paper Rex was the visible counterexample, but for years it was the kind of counterexample that came with caveats. The team made deep runs at Reykjavik. It made deep runs at Champions. It always seemed, in interviews, to be one map away from breaking through, and then the map would come and the EMEA opposition would adjust at the half and Paper Rex would lose 13-9 in a way that suggested the structure was right but the depth was not. Toronto was the event where the structure proved that the depth was, in fact, sufficient.

The mechanical core

The roster's two stars are f0rsakeN and Jinggg, and the case for both has shifted since 2024. f0rsakeN, throughout his early career, was framed as the irreplaceable initiator who lifted the team's ceiling. The framing was correct, but it understated what Jinggg has become. Jinggg's duelist play across the 2025 season, and into Toronto, is the most efficient duelist work a Pacific team has produced. He is winning entry duels at rates EMEA duelists are not winning them. He is doing it on the maps where Pacific teams have historically been weakest.

68%
Jinggg's first-blood win rate across the Toronto knockout stage · the highest individual entry rate at any VCT Masters event since 2023

The pairing matters because it lets Paper Rex run an offense that does not need to be telegraphed. Most top teams default into structured defaults that pre-arrange utility before the contact. Paper Rex defaults into a fast probe by the duelist, lets the contact tell them which side of the map is open, and rotates utility into the open side mid-round. Other teams have tried to play this way. Paper Rex is the first team in the Pacific era to consistently win bo3s with it against EMEA opposition.

The match that finally landed

The grand final at Toronto was decided in the agent draft. Paper Rex banked on a Lotus comp that had not been picked at scale by any other tier-one team. The bet was that a controller-light setup, balanced by the entry pressure of the duelist core, would deny EMEA's preferred mid-round retake structure on the post-plant. The bet worked. EMEA's coaching staff, in the post-match press conference, declined to discuss the comp choice in any detail. The map ended 13-7.

Inside the venue, the moment that probably defined the run came earlier, on map two of the upper bracket final, when Jinggg cleared a post-plant retake with a flank that the EMEA caller had not assigned utility to defend. The sequence was three rounds long. It is the sequence that, on rewatch, every Pacific coaching staff has been showing their academy rosters. The lesson is not the mechanical play. The lesson is the structural confidence that Paper Rex now plays with on neutral maps. They believe, on every round, that the field will adjust to them. They are right.

What it means for the region

The structural read of Toronto is that the Pacific region has produced its first championship-tier roster on a roster-construction model that is not derived from EMEA assumptions. That matters because every other Pacific roster, since franchising, has been quietly absorbing EMEA tape work and trying to outplay EMEA opponents at their own game. Paper Rex did the opposite. The team committed to a Pacific style and refined it until the style was good enough to win.

"When we played them in the semifinal, our prep was wrong. They didn't run any of the things we expected. By the time we adjusted, we'd lost two maps and the bracket was over." · anonymous EMEA team analyst, Toronto, on background

The downstream effect is already visible. Pacific academy programs are no longer trying to produce smaller versions of EMEA stars. Coaches in Korea and Japan have begun openly discussing roster construction patterns based on the Paper Rex model. The shift will take a season or two to surface in results. The blueprint is now public.

What's next

Champions, later this year, will be the next test. EMEA will arrive with prep specifically against the f0rsakeN-Jinggg core and against the controller-light Lotus pool. Paper Rex will arrive as a defending Masters champion with the institutional confidence that comes from having actually closed an international final. The matchup between region and region is now structurally even, perhaps for the first time in Valorant's history. What happens next is no longer a question of whether the Pacific region can produce a champion. It is a question of whether the region can defend one.
