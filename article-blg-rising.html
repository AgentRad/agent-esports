Bilibili Gaming and the LPL's Long Climb to Worlds Glory

Two domestic titles, two MSI runs, and two consecutive Worlds finals lost on the very last map. Bilibili Gaming has spent three seasons being the closest thing the LPL has to a championship-tier roster. The next year is the one that finally has to land.
By Marcus Chen · Senior Esports Reporter · 10 min read · Published April 2, 2026

The hardest thing about being the best team in your region is that the rest of the world plays seven games a year against you, and the rest of your region plays seventy. By the time the Worlds bracket comes around, the team that has had to grind through the LPL gauntlet has spent the entire summer adapting to the meta the LPL has built, while the team across the stage from them has spent the summer adapting to the meta the LCK has built. Bilibili Gaming has lived inside that asymmetry for two consecutive years now. The book on them in the Worlds finals room is, unfortunately, a long book.

That is the structural condition of the modern LPL champion, and it is the condition BLG has been trying to break out of since 2024. The team has built a roster that is, by the basic measures of individual skill, the best in the LPL. The team has built coaching infrastructure that is, by all credible inside reporting, the best in the league. The team has produced two consecutive deep international runs that finished, in both cases, on the very last map of the very last best-of-five. The trophy has not arrived. The next twelve months are about whether it does.

The roster question

The center of the BLG project is Knight, the mid laner who is, on most credible models, the second-best player in the world at his position behind only Faker. Knight's career arc is one of the more painful in modern League of Legends. He has played, across two organizations, in three Worlds finals. He has lost all three. The first two were not winnable. The third, in 2024, almost was. The fourth, if it comes, has to be.

"The thing I think about, when we lose at Worlds, is whether the practice we did in the LPL was the right practice. Sometimes I think the answer is no." · Knight, in a post-Worlds press conference, November 2024

The roster around Knight has changed at the margins season to season. The 2025 BLG version was anchored by a top side that emphasized lane priority and a bot lane that emphasized late-game scaling. The 2026 version, with one significant off-season acquisition in the bot lane, is calibrated more toward early skirmish play. The shift is partly a meta read. It is also a tacit acknowledgment that the previous version, however dominant in regional play, did not translate to the version of the game LCK and LEC opponents wanted to play in international finals.

The LPL meta problem

The deeper problem, and the one that goes beyond BLG specifically, is that the LPL plays an internal version of the game that is structurally different from the version international finals are increasingly decided on. The LPL prizes mid-game skirmish creativity. It rewards teams that can generate kills out of neutral map states. The LCK, in particular, has spent the last three years building a counter-meta that punishes those skirmishes by playing slower, soaking objectives, and forcing the LPL representative to over-extend in mid-game windows the LCK team has decided not to contest.

0-3
LPL record in best-of-five international finals against LCK opposition since the start of 2024 · with each of the three series ending in 3-2 or 3-1 LCK wins

The LPL has not failed to win these series because the talent is worse. The talent in the league is, on most projections, equal or better. The league has lost these series because the practice environment has, in effect, trained the wrong skill set. BLG, more than any other LPL roster, has been candid in its post-Worlds debriefs about this. The 2024 final loss was, by the team's own analysis, decided in pre-tournament prep, not on the day.

What changed for 2026

The visible change inside BLG, going into the 2026 cycle, is at the coaching level. The team brought in additional analytical staff with extensive scrim history against LCK rosters, and restructured the in-season scrim partner pool to include more cross-region practice. The org has been quiet about specific arrangements, but inside reporting suggests that BLG is now scrimming twice a week with at least one LEC top-tier roster and once a week with a top LCK roster. That had not been true at scale in the previous cycles.

The second visible change is in roster usage. Knight, since the start of the spring split, has played a more measured laning style. The team has moved away from the patterns where the mid laner generates leads through skirmish into a model where the mid laner is given lane priority and the team plays around it. The shift is, in effect, a domestic implementation of the LCK-style mid-game soak that has been beating BLG in finals. The team is, by intention, learning to play the game the way the team that beats them plays it.

The 2026 stakes

The window for this BLG core to win a Worlds title is real but narrowing. Knight is 23, which is no longer young by professional League standards. The bot lane is in its prime years. The top side has another full season of growth available. The team will, on most credible models, be a top-three favorite at MSI 2026 and a top-two favorite at Worlds 2026, depending on which LCK rosters peak.

"We are in our window. I think everyone in the building knows that. Last year was the year we expected to do it. This year is the year we have to." · anonymous BLG staff member, AgentTV interview, March 2026

The broader stake, beyond BLG specifically, is whether the LPL can finally close the meta gap that has cost the league three consecutive Worlds finals. If BLG, with this roster and this preparation cycle, cannot do it, the structural questions about the LPL's international ceiling will get louder. If they can, the league has its answer, and Knight has the trophy his career has been chasing for five years. The next twelve months are, for both, the moment of truth.
